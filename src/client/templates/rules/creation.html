Creating a character

When creating a character, you will have to select

  1. Race and sex
  2. A class
  3. Talents
  4. Skills
  5. Optionally, feats

Race

Sex

You may freely choose your character’s sex. It has the following effects:

  • Men receive {{ 50|bonus }} points in the Brawn and Constitution talents
  • Women receive {{ 30|bonus }} points in the Social talent

Apart from this, sex has no impact on talents, skills, classes, or anything much else (in terms of rules).

Attributes

When you create a character, you will have a certain number of talent points and skill points to spend. Since skills and talents work the same, we can call these attribute points for short. (They work exactly the same; it’s just that you can only spend talent points on talents, not skills, and vice versa.)

Skills have different levels in parent/child relationships. For instance, the Longsword skill is a Sword skill is a Hand-to-hand weapons skill, which gives us levels:

  1. Hand-to-hand weapons
  2. Sword
  3. Longsword

The most general skills are most widely applicable and harder to learn, since they represent a general knowledge of all their child skills: Obviously it’s harder to be good at wielding swords in general than it is to wield one particular kind of sword, and being good at hand-to-hand weapons in general is very difficult indeed. This is represented by a different cost for different skills:

  1. Level 1 skills (very specific): 1 point
  2. Level 2 skills (somewhat specific): 2 points
  3. Level 3 skills (most general): 3 points

This means that you can more easily get better at very specific skills; but on the other hand, these are more specific—less generally applicable.

Points and values

Every attribute has two numbers associated with it: The attribute points and attribute value. The points represent how much time, effort, training, or experience you have put into improving that attribute; the value represents how strong it is.

The relationship between the points and the value is simple: The attribute value is the square root of the attribute points. This reflects the fact that you learn most quickly early in the process of improving any skill or attribute. A beginner can learn a lot in his first few weeks and months of practice, whereas a master may have to train for years to get even better than he already is.

Parent/child relationships

Since a child skill is a specialised version of a parent skill, you can never be worse at a child skill than at a parent skill. (Going back to our standby example, it makes little sense to suggest that you’re better with swords than you are with a longsword!) This is reflected in the following rule:

When computing the value of an attribute, add to its specific points the sum of all its parent points.

Suppose you have the following point distribution:

  • Hand-to-hand weapons: 2
  • Swords: 8
  • Longsword: 5

With specific training in Longsword, your character has 5 skill points (for a skill value, in itself, of $$ \left\lfloor \sqrt{5} \right\rfloor = 2 $$ ); but added to this he has 8 points from being generally good with swords, and another 2 points from wide experience with hand-to-hand weapons. Thus the total Longsword value is $$ \left\lfloor \sqrt{5 + 8 + 2} \right\rfloor = \left\lfloor \sqrt{15} \right\rfloor = 4 $$

Note that by specifically training with a longsword, your character is better at this than had they placed their skill points in general hand-to-hand skills, because the more general skill is more expensive.

  • Hand-to-hand weapons: 2 costs 6 points
  • Swords: 8 costs 16 points
  • Longsword: 5 costs 5 points

for a total cost of 27, for 15 points in Longsword. By placing all 27 points in Hand-to-hand weapons, you would only get 9 points. By specialising entirely in Longsword you could get 27 points and a value of 5, but on the other hand be quite useless with other weapons.
