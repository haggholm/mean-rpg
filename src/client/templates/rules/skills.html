Overview

The game deals with three kinds of properties:

  • Attributes refer to those things that tend to stay fairly constant, and do not depend on knowledge—things like size, strength, agility, intelligence, and so on.
  • Skills are learned skills, including spells.
  • Modifiers are any factors that affect you. These could be situational modifiers, such as from being sick, poisoned, injured, or under the effect of a performance enhancing drug. In combat, a common modifier is the use of special tactics or techniques.

When checking for success, you will roll , add the values of the relevant skill and attribute, and any modifiers. If the result is greater than the difficulty, the roll is a success; if equal, a draw; if less, a failure.

Attributes, skills, and modifiers

Attributes

Attributes are a measure of a character’s general strengths and weaknesses. They are comprised of

  • The Power attributes, which describe a character’s physical fitness. These are the primary attributes for warriors.
    • Strength
    • Size
    • Toughness
  • The Dexterity attributes, the primary attributes for thieves.
    • Agility
    • Co-ordination
    • Quickness
  • The Mental attributes, the most important attributes for scholars and wizards.
    • Learning
    • Willpower
    • Wits
  • The Social attributes:
    • Appearance
    • Charisma
    • Perception

(Depending on how detailed you want to get, you can regard these as either four or twelve attributes.)

A character’s attributes don’t change very much over time. Although you can get stronger, faster, and so on, it’s much more difficult and less likely than simply improving skills.

Skills

Skills represent anything a character may learn: To wield a weapon, pick a lock, read a book in some particular language, or cast a spell.

Skills and attributes

Each skill is usually related to some attribute. For example, hand-to-hand combat skills depend on physical co-ordination—that is, when you’re fighting someone, that is usually the attribute of interest. For this reason, skill cards will have the chief attribute already noted on them.

However, this use is not cut in stone. Although physical co-ordination is clearly the talent of interest when fencing, there may be situations where another attribute is more relevant—for instance, if you’re trying to hack through a barrier, or hack through a purely defensive opponent’s shield by brute force and no finesse, Strength may matter more than Co-ordination. Persuasion generally relies on Charisma, but with the right strategy you could substitute Appearance—or even Size or Strength, if you’re attempting intimidation.

Use your judgement to decide which attribute is more relevant to the situation. And by all means be creative—if you can think of a reason why another attribute could be made more relevant, find a way to explain how the context allows your character to use it. However, the GM always has final say.

Modifiers: Abilities, feats, and states

Modifiers are basically “anything else” that can apply to a skill check: An unusual ability, a particular aptitude, or a temporary state that affects your chance of success. These are much less systematic than skills and talents.

Players don’t always get to pick their own modifiers. The GM will apply modifiers as necessary for the state the character is in, such as if they are wounded, affected by a performance enhancing drug, under the effect of a spell, or simply working under particularly favourable or unfavourable conditions.

Modifiers and other properties

Most modifiers are only relevant to some properties. For instance, the Reckless combat modifier applies to hand-to-hand weapons—you can fight recklessly—but it makes very little sense to recklessly read a book in secret runes. Some modifiers, such as magic spells, might apply directly to specific attributes. Thus, each modifier will specify whether it applies only to certain skills and attributes, or (as is the case for some attributes, like Injured states) to all checks.

Rolls and checks

Whenever you face a challenge where you need to use your character’s properties and may or may not succeed, we will simulate the effort by performing what’s called a check. Most checks are skill checks, though we’ll start by looking at attribute checks.

Every time you perform a skill check, you will perform it against some sort of resistance. Depending on the situation, this may be an active or passive resistance. If you’re trying to pick a lock, you’re fighting the passive resistance of the difficulty of that particular lock (represented by some number). If you’re fighting an enemy, you’re fighting the active resistance of that character—who may use the same kinds of resources (talents, skills, modifiers) as you. Either way, you’ll end up with a resistance value: either the passive resistance value, or the sum of the opponent’s talent, skill, and modifiers.

Rolling the dice

Most of the rolls in the game are performed using the ten-sided die, the d10. Moreover, they are typically unlimited rolls—we’ll borrow a convention and denote this by . This means that if you roll a 10, you may re-roll; if you roll another 10, you may re-roll again; and so on. This means that the roll value is theoretically unlimited, though in practice very high values just won’t happen unless you’re playing with loaded dice.

Attribute checks

An attribute check is a check of one of your character attributes against some resistance. For example, when you are injured, you may need to perform a Toughness check to see whether your character is overwhelmed by pain and incapacitated. Another example might be if you are attempting to pry something open by brute force, and perform a Strength check.

To perform the check, compute your check value

\begin{align} \text{ check value } = \text{ 1d10* } \pm \text{ attribute } \end{align}

If your check value is higher than the resistance, you succeed; if it is lower, you fail. If it’s equal, it’s a “draw”, the meaning of which depends on circumstances. For example, when prying something open with brute Strength, a “draw” might mean that you neither succeed nor conclusively fail at the task but may try again.

If you are checking an attribute against an opponent, the resistance is their check value.

Skill checks

Example

Terrick is fighting a duel with Mandor. Terrick has the following cards:

  • Talent: Precision {{ 2|bonus }}
  • Skill: Sidesword {{ 7|bonus }}
  • Modifiers: Injured {{ -1|bonus }}

Meanwhile Mandor plays the following:

  • Talent: Precision {{ 3|bonus }}
  • Skill: Sidesword {{ 12|bonus }}
  • Modifiers: Injured {{ -2|bonus }}

We can see that Terrick’s properties add up to a value of {{ 8|bonus }}, while Mandor’s add up to {{ 13|bonus }}—a considerable advantage. Terrick rolls

\begin{align} 2 + 7 - 1 + [ \text{1d10* } \rightarrow 9 ] = 17 \end{align}

while his opponent rolls

3+12-2+ [ \text{1d10*} \rightarrow 2 ] = 15

Terrick, with great luck, wins the exchange by a margin of 17-15=2.

Most checks are skill checks, where you are comparing a skill against a resistance. However, skills are always aided by attributes and natural aptitude—given equal technical skill, people with naturally good co-ordination will fight better; a more agile person will climb better. This is reflected in that you get to count an attribute value as well as your skill value. To perform a skill check, roll and add your stats:

\begin{align*} \text{ check value } & = \text{ 1d10* } \\ & \pm \text{ skill value } \\ & \pm \text{ attribute } \\ & \pm \text{ modifiers } \end{align*}

Compare this to the difficulty or resistance value. If you are contending with an active opponent, this will be a similar roll. If your check value is greater than the resistance, your check is successful. If it’s less, your check fails. If the values are equal, it’s a draw. Often, the margin is meaningful, so take note of it.

Note that most of the time, a skill is associated with a specific, given attribute, so rather than have to add them up every time you roll, you’ll note the sum on your card and just roll

\begin{align*} \text{ check value } & = \text{ 1d10* } \\ & \pm \text{ skill and attribute value } \\ & \pm \text{ modifiers } \end{align*}

And since there often are no modifiers, it boils down to

\begin{align*} \text{ check value } & = \text{ 1d10* } \\ & \pm \text{ skill and attribute value } \\ \end{align*}
Notes

Very roughly, given equally matched opponents,

  • 50% of the time, the difference is ≤3.
  • 75% of the time, the difference is ≤7.
  • 90% of the time, the difference is ≤10.

Substitutions

We briefly mentioned earlier that you can substitute one property for another. For example, supposing you are adept with the longsword, but are forced to fight with nothing to hand but an arming sword, you’ll surely have some idea of what to do with it—although you’re unlikely to match someone specifically trained with the sidesword. Indeed, a truly masterful warrior may well be able to pick up just about any weapon with at least some proficiency.

This is reflected in that skills are presented in hierarchies, such as “Hand-to-hand combat » Sword » Longsword”. For each “level” away from your own skill, you suffer a {{-2|bonus}} penalty. Thus with a Longsword skill of 6, you can wield any sword at a level of 4, and any hand-to-hand weapon with a value of 2.

Taking your time, and skills that don’t require rolls

When you can afford to take your time, and when a skill check is not a contest against an active opponent, it doesn’t make very much sense to require a roll of the dice. Under such circumstances, you may simply assume that you roll the average outcome, \text{ 1d10* } \rightarrow 6.

With some skills, it rarely if ever makes sense to roll. For example, language skills serve to measure how good you are at speaking a language—there’s not much room for an element of chance. However, even with such skills there may be special circumstances where you want to roll—to see whether you can translate the arcane runes in the brief moment before you must flee; or when facing an opponent in a contest to compose the better rhetoric. You should use your judgement (in particular, the GM should!) to decide when it makes sense to roll.

Points and values

Each property has two values: A number of points representing how much you have “put into” it in terms of training, experience, and so on, and its value, representing how good you are at it.

Difficulty

Math nerds only

If \text{difficulty } > 0, then we need to use

\begin{align*} {value} = \left\lfloor \frac{\sqrt{8 * points + 1}-1}{2} \right\rfloor - {difficulty} \end{align*}

Some skills can be attempted by pretty much anyone, though odds are that most people won’t be any good at them until they gain experience. Other skills require training before they can be used at all. Anyone can pick up a sword and swing it, however wildly and crudely, and in fact even the completely untrained can kill with a sword—though they will be swiftly cut down by an adept. On the other hand, one completely untutored in a language has no chance at all of speaking it.

To reflect this, some skills have a difficulty associated with them. This is to be subtracted from your skill value. It is rather as if, with a skill with difficulty 2, you start with a value of {{-2|bonus}}, and need to get 3 points worth of training just to work your way up to 0.

In order to use a skill, you must have at least a value of 0 in it, reflecting the kind of vague grasp of the concepts required to attempt it and learn by trial and error. Thus, for skills with difficulty 0, anyone can attempt them and eventually learn. For skills with higher difficulty, you’ll need training before you can use them at all. This value refers to the plain skill value, before applying any attributes or modifiers.

Skill trees

Some skills are intrinsically connected, and expertise at one will translate into competence with another. For instance, a master of the longsword will not be helpless with a sidesword; a blacksmith has some idea of goldsmithing; a scholar deeply learned in statecraft and politics will pick up some facts of geography.

This is reflected in skills being organised in trees: A skill is a member of a skill group, which is part of a still more general skill category. You cannot specifically train or learn the “parent” skills—the groups and categories. Instead, they correspond to the general knowledge you gain of a broader subject as you study its specifics.

As an example, the skill category Unarmed Combat contains skill groups such as Sword and Polearm. Meanwhile, the Sword group contains specific skills like Longsword and Sidesword.

Math nerds only

In other words, the parent points are

\begin{align*} \text{group points} = \left\lfloor \frac{\text{skill points}}{3} \right\rfloor \end{align*}

The same relationship holds for group-to-category, so

\begin{align*} \text{category points} &= \left\lfloor \frac{\text{group points}}{3} \right\rfloor \\ &= \left\lfloor \frac{\text{skill points}}{9} \right\rfloor \end{align*}

In the rules, this is reflected as follows:

  • When placing points in a specific skill, every third point goes to the skill group instead.
  • When thus placing points in a skill group, every third point goes to the category instead.
  • You don’t get to count the same point twice. Thas is, if you place 3 points in a skill, you get 1 point in its skill group, but you then effectively subtract it from the skill itself.

If you only have one skill under a category (for example, you are trained in the use of only one kind of weapon), you can mostly ignore this, since the value ends up being precisely the same anyway.

This means that a character who entirely lacks training in a given skill may be able to use it, may even be quite good at it, simply because he is trained in a similar one. A master of the longsword may not excel with the sidesword, but will nonetheless easily defeat the untrained. Hand him even a polearm, and though the techniques are very different, he will nonetheless build on a solid understanding of measure, timing, and body mechanics.

Attribute trees

Attribute trees work for attributes exactly as skill trees do for skills, with the sole exception that there are only two levels: Rather than skills, groups, and categories there are attributes and attribute categories.

Improving values

Every time you use a skill or face a challenge, you stand a chance of improving one of your values.

  • If you roll a double 10 (roll for 10, re-roll, and roll another 10), you gain an experience point in the skill used.
  • If you roll a triple 10, you also gain a point to the attribute used. Obviously, this is extremely rare. It also doesn’t apply to all attributes—you may get stronger from using skills that rely on strength, but you will not grow larger from using skills that exploit size.
